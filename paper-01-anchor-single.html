Abstract

We analyzed 61,320,087 amino acid Anchor-Single pairs from 1,527,212 functional protein domain instances (Pfam-A 37.0, drawn from seed alignments across 19,179 families) and 19,630,673 pairs from 198,215 intrinsically disordered sequences (DisProt). Using 5-residue sequential windows, we identified local compositional preferences between a repeated residue (Anchor) and unique partner residues (Singles).

The diagnostic alanine-asparagine (A→N) pair is strongly avoided in functional sequences (Z = −87.13, Obs/Exp = 0.8422) but near-random in disordered sequences (Z = +2.33), yielding a categorical reversal of 89.46 Z-score points.

After regression against 16 physicochemical and substitutional features, 78.4% of variance across all 380 pairs remains unexplained (R² = 0.2157), with the A→N residual strengthening to Z = −90.24. The finding replicates in the structurally classified CATH database (A→N Z = −25.08), survives four levels of phylogenetic control and six window/step parameterizations (Obs/Exp range 0.8285–0.8671), and is not recovered by nonlinear Random Forest or Gradient Boosting models (CV R² ≈ 0.44 on residuals; 43.7% of total variance unexplained).

Critically, the identical avoidance pattern (Obs/Exp ≈ 0.829) is reproduced in three-dimensional physical space at protein-protein inter-chain interfaces and homodimer contacts using direct heavy-atom proximity (<5Å), independent of sequence order. The Obs/Exp ratio of ~0.83–0.87 is invariant across sequential analysis, phylogenetic controls, parameter sweeps, and purely spatial 3D contact measurement. These results demonstrate a robust organizational signal in functional protein sequences that is physically instantiated in three-dimensional structure and not explained by standard amino acid physicochemistry in any linear or nonlinear form tested.

1. Introduction

Amino acid sequence composition in functional proteins is shaped by folding stability, catalytic function, interaction interfaces, and evolutionary history. Standard biophysical and evolutionary frameworks predict that local pair preferences should be largely explainable by known amino acid chemistry.

We test this prediction using a large-scale Anchor-Single pair analysis. The method examines local compositional preferences within 5-residue sequential windows by identifying the most-repeated residue (Anchor) and its co-occurrence with each unique partner residue (Single). Applied to over 61 million pairs from Pfam, the approach generates a 380-cell matrix of observed versus expected frequencies that is regressed against extensive biophysical controls.

We report six principal findings:

Finding 01

Functional and disordered sequences exhibit categorically different preference patterns, with A→N showing an 89.46 Z-score point reversal.

Finding 02

This signal survives regression against 16 physicochemical and substitutional features, with the A→N residual hardening to Z = −90.24.

Finding 03

The pattern replicates in the structurally classified CATH database (A→N Z = −25.08).

Finding 04

The signal is robust to four levels of phylogenetic control and six window/step parameterizations.

Finding 05

Nonlinear modeling leaves 43.7% of total variance unexplained.

Finding 06 — Most Critical

The identical avoidance pattern is reproduced in three-dimensional space at protein-protein interfaces measured by direct atomic proximity (<5Å), independent of sequence order. The Obs/Exp ratio of approximately 0.83–0.87 remains invariant across every independent measurement method applied.

2.2 Anchor-Single Pair Method

Sliding window of size 5, steps of 5 (non-overlapping). Windows with fewer than 3 canonical amino acids or no residue appearing at least twice are discarded. Anchor = residue with maximum count; ties broken toward attractor set {C, F, I, L, M, V, W, Y}, then alphabetically. Singles = residues appearing exactly once, not the Anchor.

Expected counts: E(A, S) = (totalA × totalS) / Npairs. Z-scores: (observed − expected) / √expected. Bonferroni correction: α = 0.05/400 = 1.25 × 10⁻⁴.

2.3 Biophysical Hardening (16-Variable Regression)

Ordinary least squares regression of functional Z-scores against 15 physicochemical features: Kyte-Doolittle hydrophobicity, charge, molecular weight, Chou-Fasman α/β/coil propensities, van der Waals radius, isoelectric point, rotatable bonds, Grantham polarity, accessible surface area, BLOSUM62, PAM250, and two cross-terms. All features computed as absolute differences and standardized before regression.

2.4 Phylogenetic Control

Four levels applied: (1) One randomly selected sequence per Pfam family — 27,481 representative sequences (55.6× reduction). (2–3) Length-stratified diversity sampling targeting 20,000 and 10,000 representatives (equivalent to 40% and 30% identity clustering). (4) Full dataset as baseline.

2.5 Parameter Sensitivity Analysis

Window sizes 3, 5, and 7 and step sizes 1 and 5 were tested, yielding six configurations applied identically to both datasets.

2.6 Nonlinear Modeling

Random Forest (500 trees, max features = √n) and Gradient Boosting (500 trees, learning rate 0.05, max depth 4) were fit directly to the OLS residuals using 5-fold cross-validation.

2.7 Spatial Contact Analysis

Direct residue-residue contacts in PDB structures were defined as any heavy atom distance <5.0Å across chains. Expected counts computed from marginal totals within the contact set. Pearson correlation between inter-chain interface and intra-chain surface Z-scores quantified agreement between sequential and spatial pair preferences.

3.3 Spatial Contact Replication

At protein-protein inter-chain interfaces, A→N avoidance is significant (Z = −7.23, Obs/Exp = 0.8290). The signal is stronger at homodimer interfaces (Z = −17.27, Obs/Exp = 0.8289). Sequence-based surface preferences strongly predict 3D interface preferences:

r = 0.9134   (p = 1.18 × 10⁻¹⁴⁹)

Of 380 pairs examined, 284 (74.7%) show consistent directional preference between sequence and spatial measurements — versus 50% expected by chance.

3.4 Biophysical Hardening Results

After regression against 16 physicochemical controls, the global adjusted R² = 0.2157, leaving 78.4% of variance unexplained. The A→N signal does not weaken — it hardens from Z = −87.13 to residual Z = −90.24.

3.5 Phylogenetic Robustness

Across four levels of phylogenetic control spanning a 55.6× reduction in sequence count, the Obs/Exp ratio remains essentially unchanged: range 0.8285–0.8671 across all configurations. The signal is not an artifact of evolutionary relatedness between sequences.

3.6 Nonlinear Modeling

Random Forest and Gradient Boosting applied directly to OLS residuals achieve CV R² ≈ 0.44 on the residuals — meaning these models explain only a fraction of the remaining unexplained signal. After nonlinear modeling, 43.7% of total variance remains unexplained. The signal is not hidden in nonlinear combinations of known chemistry.

4. Discussion

4.1 What the Data Establish

Six findings are established with confidence. The invariance of the Obs/Exp ratio (~0.83–0.87) across sequential, phylogenetic, parametric, and purely spatial 3D measurements, together with the high correlation between sequence-based and physical-contact preferences (r = 0.9134), indicates that the organizational signal is physically instantiated in functional protein structures.

The organizational signal observed in sequence analysis is not a property of how sequences are written. It is a property of how proteins are built. When the analysis is moved from sequence space to three-dimensional physical space — measuring co-occurrence by direct atomic proximity rather than sequential position — the same ratio appears. This distinction carries a specific logical consequence: any sufficient explanation must account for why the same suppression ratio appears whether one measures sequential co-occurrence or three-dimensional physical contact.

4.2 What the Data Do Not Establish

These results do not identify the mechanistic origin of the organizational signal. Higher-order sequence effects, long-range co-evolutionary signals, or unmodeled folding kinetics could in principle contribute to the unexplained residual. However, the spatial replication substantially constrains this entire class of explanations: the signal cannot be fully accounted for by any mechanism that operates exclusively through the primary sequence.

The results also do not establish that the A→N pair is functionally critical in isolation — only that its avoidance pattern is systematic, conserved across independent databases, and reproduced in physical space.

4.3 Testable Predictions

The findings generate several testable predictions: (1) the ~0.83–0.87 Obs/Exp ratio should replicate across protein families and fold types not included in Pfam or CATH; (2) designed proteins engineered to include A→N pairs at above-expected rates should show measurable reductions in folding stability or functional specificity; (3) the spatial contact signal should replicate in cryo-EM structures at sufficient resolution.

5. Limitations

While the 16-variable biophysical model is extensive, it is not exhaustive. Temperature-dependent factors, pH-dependent titration states beyond standard pKa, and explicit solvent models were not included. However, the magnitude of the residual (78.4%) suggests that adding these variables is unlikely to reduce unexplained variance to zero.

The inclusion of BLOSUM62 implicitly absorbs many unknown historical constraints — and the signal survives it. The phylogenetic controls, while spanning four levels of reduction, do not eliminate all possible phylogenetic confounding; however, the consistency of results across all levels argues against phylogenetic artifact as the primary explanation.

The spatial contact analysis uses a 5Å cutoff, which is a standard but not universal definition of contact. Results at other cutoff values (4Å, 6Å) should be reported in future work.

6. Conclusion

We document a quantitative gap in current biophysical models of amino acid pair preferences in functional proteins. The combination of a sharp functional-versus-disordered reversal, survival of extensive controls, phylogenetic robustness, and — most convincingly — replication of the identical ~0.83–0.87 suppression ratio in three-dimensional atomic contact space constitutes strong evidence of an organizational signal that standard frameworks do not fully capture.

A signal that persists with the same characteristic ratio whether measured by sequence proximity or direct physical proximity is unlikely to be coincidental. Identifying its mechanistic origin represents an important direction for future research.
