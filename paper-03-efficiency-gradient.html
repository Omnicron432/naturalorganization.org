Abstract

Analysis of 2,000 PDB structures, with filtered subsets of obligate homodimers (80,029 interface pairs) and SIFTS-confirmed enzyme interfaces (138,999 interface pairs), reveals a directionally consistent pattern of organizational signal across hierarchical levels.

The diagnostic pair A→N shows Z-scores of −87.13 at the sequence level (Pfam functional domains), −8.80 at enzyme non-interface surfaces, and −2.91 at enzyme protein-protein interfaces. After simultaneous ordinary least-squares regression against six biophysical and substitutional controls, 97.7% of the A→N signal at the sequence level remains, demonstrating near-complete orthogonality to known amino acid chemistry.

The directional consistency across independent measurement approaches — signal strongest where organizational rules are established, attenuating where they are executed — is consistent with the Functional Inheritance Model proposed here, in which sequence-level grammar pre-positions viable organizational solutions that are inherited and utilized at the structural level. This model generates testable predictions including rapid adaptive responses on ecological timescales through selection among pre-existing grammatical possibilities.

1. Introduction — The Inverted Gradient

Standard accounts of molecular evolution assume that functional constraint intensifies at sites of direct biological function: protein-protein interfaces and enzyme active sites. Under this view, sequence-level patterns are presumed to be weak, diffuse, or merely neutral background.

The present analysis tests this assumption directly across three organizational levels using independent measurement approaches. The conventional prediction is straightforward:

LevelConventional Prediction
InterfaceStrongest constraint
SurfaceModerate constraint
SequenceWeakest constraint

The data presented here are inconsistent with this prediction. The organizational signal is strongest at the sequence level and attenuates at the structural levels. This pattern is observed across all subsets tested and is interpreted as consistent with an alternative model — the Functional Inheritance Model — described in Section 5.

3.3 Orthogonality to Known Biophysics

After simultaneous regression against six physicochemical and substitutional controls, 97.7% of the sequence-level A→N signal remains (R² = 0.0995). The residual Z-score of −87.33 after controls essentially equals the raw value of −87.13, confirming near-complete orthogonality to all tested biophysical properties.

4. The Functional Inheritance Model

The Functional Inheritance Model is proposed as an interpretation consistent with the data rather than a conclusion proven by it. It makes specific testable predictions that distinguish it from the standard gradualist account.

4.1 Core Propositions

Proposition 1 — Non-Redundant Hierarchy

Organizational constraint is established at the sequence level and inherited downstream. Interfaces utilize pre-positioned materials rather than regenerating the grammar independently.

Proposition 2 — Pre-Positioned Feedstock

The 97.7% residual signal after physicochemical controls demonstrates that viable organizational solutions exist within the sequence-level grammar. Whether this constitutes a pre-positioned feedstock for adaptation is an empirical question the model generates, not a conclusion the current data establishes.

Proposition 3 — Efficiency Through Inheritance

The directional pattern — stronger signal upstream, attenuating downstream — is consistent with a non-redundant hierarchical system. The same pattern could arise from other mechanisms including differential selective pressure across organizational levels.

4.2 Standard Model vs. Functional Inheritance Model

Standard Gradualist Account

  • Strongest constraint at interfaces
  • Interfaces generate novel grammar
  • Adaptation requires new mutations
  • Incremental, memoryless variation

Functional Inheritance Model

  • Strongest constraint at sequence level
  • Interfaces inherit pre-existing grammar
  • Adaptation may reveal latent grammatical possibilities
  • Variation on pre-structured substrate

5. Discussion

5.1 What the Data Establish

Three findings are established with confidence. First, sequence-level amino acid pair preferences contain structured signal that is 97.7% orthogonal to six standard physicochemical controls at the diagnostic A→N pair. Second, this signal shows a directionally consistent pattern of attenuation from sequence to surface to interface across all subsets tested. Third, the preference architecture is coherent across structural levels (Pearson r = 0.923 between interface and surface Z-scores), indicating that the same pairs are generally preferred and avoided at both levels.

5.2 What the Data Do Not Establish

The data do not establish that the gradient reflects causal inheritance of organizational grammar from sequence to structure. The measurements come from methodologically distinct analyses and the directional pattern, while consistent, could reflect differential selective pressure, sampling differences, or other factors not captured by the current framework. The Functional Inheritance Model is a coherent interpretation of the pattern; it is not the only interpretation.

5.3 Interpretive Caution on Interface Concentration

The preference concentration at interfaces is dominated by charge-complementarity pairs (E↔R, D↔R, E↔K, D↔K), which are well-characterized biophysical interactions. The biophysical regression explains 51.3% of interface signal, substantially more than the 10% explained at the sequence level. The interface concentration finding is real but reflects known chemistry becoming dominant as well as any organizational inheritance effect.

6. Testable Predictions

The Functional Inheritance Model generates several testable predictions that distinguish it from the standard account:

Prediction 01

The 97.7% residual should replicate across independent protein families and fold types not included in Pfam or Interactome3D.

Prediction 02

Interfaces that are evolutionarily young should show weaker organizational signal than ancient interfaces.

Prediction 03

The directional pattern should hold in datasets independent of Pfam and Interactome3D.

Prediction 04 — Ecological Timescale Adaptation

If sequence-level grammar pre-positions viable organizational solutions, adaptive responses to environmental change should be observable on ecological timescales without requiring new mutations. Darwin's finches — where beak morphology changes measurably within one to two generations following environmental pressure — are consistent with this prediction. Whether this reflects selection among pre-existing grammatical possibilities remains to be established through direct empirical investigation.

7. Conclusion

The Anchor-Single pair analyses reveal a directionally consistent pattern across sequence and structural levels: organizational signal is strongest at the sequence level and attenuates progressively at structural levels. The sequence-level signal is 97.7% orthogonal to six biophysical controls at the diagnostic A→N pair.

These findings are consistent with the Functional Inheritance Model, in which sequence-level grammar pre-positions viable organizational solutions that are inherited and utilized at the structural level. The data do not identify the ultimate source of the sequence-level grammar, nor do they prove that the gradient reflects causal inheritance rather than other mechanisms.

They demonstrate that functional protein sequences operate under a structured, non-random framework that is strongest at the sequence level and that current physicochemical models are substantially insufficient to account for. The methodological contributions — the Anchor-Single pair framework, the biophysical hardening procedure, and the cross-level directional analysis — provide tools for further investigation into the origin and maintenance of biological organizational grammar.
